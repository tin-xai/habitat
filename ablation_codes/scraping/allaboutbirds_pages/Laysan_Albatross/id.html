Laysan Albatross Photos and Videos

Adult/immature

Very large seabird with narrow, pointed wings. Long, thick, hooked bill is pale. Head and rump white, with dark tail and dark shading on the face.

© Nancy Christensen | Macaulay LibraryHawaii, May 27, 2017

Adult/immature

Very large seabird with narrow, pointed wings and distinctive looping flight style; rarely flaps wings. Underwings are patchy white with variable amounts of dark. Grayish smudge on face.

© Richard MacIntosh | Macaulay LibraryAlaska, June 07, 2017

Adult

Nests on the ground on islands in the Hawaiian island chain and off Mexico; flies to the Gulf of Alaska to forage. Chicks take about 6 months to fledge.

© Benjamin Clock | Macaulay LibraryHawaii, January 23, 2014

Adult/immature

Forages at sea, either by landing and dabbling, or by picking food items from the surface in flight. Note all-brown back and grayish wash on face.

© Tim Lenz | Macaulay LibraryCalifornia, August 06, 2010

Juvenile

Chicks are wispy brown with pale or blond highlights and a dark bill. Laysan Albatrosses nest among short vegetation, mainly in the remote Northwest Hawaiian Islands; also small colonies on Kauai and Oahu and on a few Mexican Islands.

© Jason Van | Macaulay LibraryHawaii, April 02, 2017

Breeding adult

Feeds at sea on squid and fish as well as flying-fish eggs and other items attached to floating debris.

© Timothy Barksdale | Macaulay LibraryCalifornia, February 01, 1997

Juvenile

Older chicks tend to retain fuzzy brown head and neck down while molting into flight feathers before fledging.

© Noah Frade | Macaulay LibraryHawaii, June 13, 2017

Adults/immatures with Northern Fulmars and orca

Spends most of the year at sea, sometimes in flocks especially around fishing boats. More likely to sit on the sea surface during calm winds when dynamic soaring is more difficult.

© Vicki Sandage | Macaulay LibraryAlaska, June 05, 2017

Adult/immature

Spends most of the year on the open ocean. Characteristic looping flight style takes advantage of prevailing winds to fly while rarely flapping wings.

© Matt Brady | Macaulay LibraryCalifornia, July 31, 2010

The Four Keys to ID

  • Size & Shape

    Laysan Albatrosses are very large seabirds (though they are among the smaller albatrosses). They have very long, very narrow wings. The neck is thick and the head is large.

    Relative Size

    Slightly smaller, more slender, and narrower-winged than a Brown Pelican; much larger than a Western Gull.

    goose-sized or larger
    Measurements
    • Both Sexes
      • Length: 31.1-31.9 in (79-81 cm)
      • Weight: 77.6-151.7 oz (2200-4300 g)
      • Wingspan: 76.8-79.9 in (195-203 cm)
    © Nancy Christensen | Macaulay Library
  • Color Pattern

    Laysan Albatrosses are white-headed birds with dark gray-brown upperwings and mostly white underwings (with variable dark markings). The underparts are clean white. They have a dark patch around the eye. In flight, note the dark back, white rump, and dark tail.

    © Richard MacIntosh | Macaulay Library
  • Behavior

    They fly by dynamic soaring: gliding low over the waves and then wheeling up into the sky to take advantage of the wind. They rarely flap their wings. They feed by sitting on the water, often at night, catching squid and other small prey with their bills.

    © Tim Lenz | Macaulay Library
  • Habitat

    Laysan Albatrosses spend most of their time on the open Pacific Ocean, spanning tropical waters up to the southern Bering Sea. They nest on open, sandy or grassy islands, mostly in the Hawaiian Island chain.

    © Matt Brady | Macaulay Library

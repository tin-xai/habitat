The Four Keys to ID

  • Size & Shape

    A heavy-bodied, very large kingfisher, with a thick, daggerlike bill, shaggy crest, fairly long tail, moderately long wings, and short legs.

    Relative Size

    Larger than a Belted Kingfisher, smaller than an American Crow.

    between robin and crow
    Measurements
    • Both Sexes
      • Length: 11.0-13.8 in (28-35 cm)
      • Weight: 4.9-6.0 oz (140-170 g)
      • Wingspan: 25.0 in (63.5 cm)
    © Brian Sullivan | Macaulay Library
  • Color Pattern

    Males are slaty blue above and rich chestnut below, with a white collar and undertail coverts and white spotting in tail and wings. Female are similar but have a broad bluish band across the breast and a thin white crescent above the chestnut belly.

    © Carlos Sanchez | Macaulay Library
  • Behavior

    Perches high above water, then dives to capture fish in the bill. When perched, often jerks the head and cocks tail upward. Hovers less than Belted Kingfisher.

    © Jay McGowan | Macaulay Library
  • Habitat

    Freshwater sites with clear or mostly clear water, including rivers, ponds, lakes, and resacas (oxbow lakes); less often in saltwater and brackish environments.

    © Bert Wessling | Macaulay Library

Regional Differences

Of the three similar subspecies recognized by ornithologists, only torquatus occurs in the United States. It is paler above and longer billed than the two subspecies found south (stictipennis) and east (stellata) of its range, which also differ in the extent of white markings in the wings and dark markings in the undertail coverts.

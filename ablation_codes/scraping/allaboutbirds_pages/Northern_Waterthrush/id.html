The Four Keys to ID

  • Size & Shape

    A large but trim warbler with a short, fine bill, a rather short tail, and long legs. The bill is smaller than Louisiana Waterthrush’s.

    Relative Size

    Larger than a Yellow Warbler, smaller than an Eastern Bluebird.

    sparrow-sized or smaller
    Measurements
    • Both Sexes
      • Length: 4.7-5.5 in (12-14 cm)
      • Weight: 0.5-0.7 oz (14.7-19.2 g)
      • Wingspan: 8.9-10.0 in (22.5-25.5 cm)
    © Paul Tavares | Macaulay Library
  • Color Pattern

    Brownish above, with a prominent pale eyebrow (supercilium); pale yellowish or buff below, with dark streaks. The legs are duskier and less pink than Louisiana Waterthrush. The supercilium stripe is thinner in Northern Waterthrush than in Louisiana, especially at rear, and is faintly yellowish.

    © Steven Mlodinow | Macaulay Library
  • Behavior

    Northern Waterthrushes forage near or on the ground or in shallow water, where their long legs enable them to wade into pond edges in pursuit of prey. They sing mostly from low vegetation, often from a concealed perch.

    © Timothy Barksdale | Macaulay Library
  • Habitat

    Northern Waterthrushes are almost always seen near still or stagnant water, even during migration. Wooded swamps, ponds with brushy edges, bogs, beaver ponds, and similar settings attract breeders and migrants, while wintering birds use mangroves.

    © Steve Bayer | Macaulay Library
